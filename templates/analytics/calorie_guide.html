Guide

Know your calories

A plain-language guide to using your calorie estimate well.

1

What are maintenance calories?

Your maintenance calories are the estimated number of calories you need each day to keep your current body weight. At maintenance, the calories you eat roughly equal the calories your body uses.

This is only an estimate, not a fixed number. Your real needs shift with body weight, daily movement, training volume, and lifestyle. The best way to check it is to track your intake and your body-weight trend for 2–3 weeks.

2

How maintenance calories are calculated

First, your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is estimated — the energy your body uses at rest for essential functions like breathing and circulation.

BMR is then adjusted for your activity level to estimate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE), which is your maintenance target:

  • BMR = energy used at rest
  • TDEE = BMR + energy from daily activity and exercise
3

Calorie deficit — for fat loss

A calorie deficit means eating fewer calories than your body uses. Over time, that generally reduces body weight. Use one when your goal is losing fat or improving body composition through a fat-loss phase.

A practical starting point is roughly a 10–20% deficit below maintenance. A moderate deficit is easier to sustain than an aggressive one, and it better protects training performance and muscle.

4

How much to lose

Rather than fixating on a calorie number, watch your body-weight trend. A practical target during fat loss is about 0.5–1.0% of body weight per week (for an 80 kg person, roughly 0.4–0.8 kg/week).

Leaner people generally do better at the slower end. Never judge progress from a single weigh-in — weight fluctuates with water, food, and sodium. Use your weekly average instead.

5

Adjusting during fat loss

Don't cut calories on a schedule. Adjust only when your progress says to:

  1. Start with a moderate deficit.
  2. Track intake consistently.
  3. Weigh in regularly under similar conditions.
  4. Use your weekly average body weight.
  5. Compare the trend across 2–3 weeks.

If weight is dropping at a good rate, keep calories the same. If it hasn't meaningfully moved for 2–3 weeks despite good adherence, consider cutting about 100–200 kcal/day. If you're losing too fast and training or energy is suffering, add about 100–200 kcal/day.

6

Calorie surplus — for muscle gain

A calorie surplus means eating more than your body uses. Combined with resistance training and enough protein, it supports muscle growth. Use one when your goal is gaining muscle or a dedicated building phase.

A practical starting point is roughly a 5–15% surplus above maintenance. A bigger surplus does not build muscle faster — the extra mostly becomes fat.

7

How much to gain

Aim for gradual gain: about 0.25–0.5% of body weight per week (for a 70 kg person, roughly 0.18–0.35 kg/week). More experienced lifters should target the slower end. Not all of the gain will be muscle — the aim is enough energy to grow while limiting fat gain.

8

Adjusting during muscle gain

Start with a small-to-moderate surplus and watch your weekly average. If you're gaining at the desired rate, hold calories steady. If weight hasn't moved for 2–3 weeks, add about 100–200 kcal/day. If you're gaining too fast, drop about 100–200 kcal/day. Adjust from your actual trend, not the calendar.

9

When to eat at maintenance

Maintenance suits holding your current weight, taking a break from prolonged dieting, or focusing on performance. Beginners and people returning to training can often still improve body composition around maintenance when training and protein are on point.

10

The most important rule

No calculator can find your exact maintenance calories — the result is a starting point. Your real-world weight trend is the feedback that refines it:

Estimate maintenance → choose your goal → set calories → follow it consistently → track weight → use weekly averages → watch the trend for 2–3 weeks → adjust by 100–200 kcal only when needed. The goal isn't to keep changing your target; it's to find an intake that works and keep it.

Quick guide

  • Maintain around maintenance calories
  • Lose fat ~10–20% below maintenance
  • Build muscle ~5–15% above maintenance
Important. These calorie figures are estimates and should be adjusted from your real progress. Short-term weight swings are normal, so don't change calories over a single weigh-in. This is general fitness and nutrition guidance — not a substitute for personalised advice from a qualified healthcare professional or registered dietitian, especially if you have a medical condition, a history of disordered eating, or special nutritional needs.
